# E-E-A-T Reference Guide
## Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines — Distilled

Source: Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines (Copyright 2025, Section 3.4 and related)

---

## 1. The E-E-A-T Framework

E-E-A-T stands for **Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust**. All four are important in Page Quality (PQ) rating, but they are not equal.

**Trust is the central, most important dimension.** The other three (E-E-A) exist to support your assessment of Trust. Untrustworthy pages have low E-E-A-T regardless of how experienced, expert, or authoritative they appear.

> "A financial scam is untrustworthy, even if the content creator is a highly experienced and expert scammer who is considered the go-to on running scams."

The dimensions can overlap: someone may develop Expertise in a topic through accumulated first-hand Experience over time. Different combinations of E-E-A are relevant to different topics and page types.

---

## 2. Definitions of Each Dimension

### Experience
Consider the extent to which the **content creator has the necessary first-hand or life experience** for the topic. Many types of pages are trustworthy and achieve their purpose well when created by people with a wealth of personal experience.

Core question: *Has this person actually done/used/lived what they're writing about?*

Example test: A product review from someone who personally used the product vs. a "review" by someone who has not.

### Expertise
Consider the extent to which the **content creator has the necessary knowledge or skill** for the topic. Different topics require different levels and types of expertise to be trustworthy.

Core question: *Does this person have the knowledge or skill required to create accurate, satisfying content on this topic?*

Example test: Home electrical rewiring advice from a skilled electrician vs. from an antique homes enthusiast with no knowledge of electrical wiring.

### Authoritativeness
Consider the extent to which **the content creator or the website is known as a go-to source** for the topic. While most topics do not have one official authoritative source, when they do, that source is often among the most reliable and trustworthy.

Core question: *Is this the recognized, go-to source for this type of information?*

Examples: A local business's own social media page for current sales. The official government page for passport renewal.

### Trust (Most Important)
Consider the extent to which the page is **accurate, honest, safe, and reliable**. The type and amount of Trust needed depends on the page type:

- **Online stores**: need secure payment systems and reliable customer service
- **Product reviews**: should be honest, written to help others make informed decisions (not solely to sell)
- **Informational pages on YMYL topics**: must be accurate to prevent harm
- **Social media posts on non-YMYL topics**: may not need a high level of Trust when the purpose is entertainment and the content doesn't risk harm

Trust is also undermined by **conflict of interest**: reviews by the product manufacturer ("Our product is great!") or paid influencers are not as trustworthy as independent reviews.

---

## 3. How to Assess Experience

**Signals of genuine first-hand experience:**
- Personal photos, original video footage, or documentation that is unique to the creator
- Specific, concrete details that only come from having actually done something (e.g., describing exactly how a product performed in their specific situation)
- Comparative judgments grounded in personal use ("I tried X and Y; here's what I found")
- Posts describing difficult personal challenges, coping strategies, or life events
- Forum posts where participants share their own results, mistakes, or learnings

**Signals of lacking first-hand experience:**
- Reviews paraphrased or summarized from other sources with no original contribution
- Generic descriptions that could apply to any product/service without specifics
- A restaurant review written by someone who has never eaten there
- Vacuum cleaner reviews summarized from other sources with no added effort or original experience
- Content that reads like it was assembled from research rather than lived practice

**Key nuance for YMYL topics**: On clear YMYL topics, sharing first-hand life experience (e.g., personal tips for sleeping while pregnant, forum discussions on coping with cancer treatment) can be high E-E-A-T as long as it is trustworthy, safe, and consistent with well-established expert consensus. However, first-hand experience cannot substitute for expert knowledge when the content is giving medical, financial, or legal advice.

**Everyday experience examples that qualify:**
- Two engineers making a humorous video about their cat ownership: their experience with cats is sufficient E-E-A-T for that purpose
- A blogger who extensively experimented with a chocolate chip cookie recipe: documented experience = high E-E-A-T
- Forum participants sharing how long a loved one lived after a cancer diagnosis: experience, not medical advice, and valuable for that purpose
- Ballet dancers on a dance forum sharing how they wash their shoes: high E-E-A-T through personal practice

---

## 4. How to Assess Expertise

### Formal vs. Everyday Expertise

Expertise does not require formal credentials on all topics. The guidelines explicitly state: "We are not just talking about formal expertise. Informal expertise is equally important, and for some topics may be a more common type of E-E-A-T."

| Topic Type | Relevant Expertise |
|---|---|
| Medical advice | Licensed physicians, medical researchers, peer-reviewed publications |
| Tax forms / financial advice | CPAs, tax attorneys, certified financial planners |
| Legal advice | Licensed attorneys, bar-admitted lawyers |
| Cooking / recipes | Experienced home cooks, professional chefs — formal credentials not required |
| Knitting / hobbies | People with 20+ years of practice — formal credentials not required |
| Humor / satire | Talent and audience recognition; no formal credentials needed |
| Home improvement tips | Skilled tradespeople; enthusiasts with real practice |
| Forum discussions on hobbies | Niche expertise visible in the quality of the posts themselves |

**How expertise level shows up in content:**
- Technical accuracy and depth beyond what a general reader would know
- Awareness of edge cases, common mistakes, or nuanced considerations
- Clear, confident explanation of complex processes
- Visible through the MC itself (you can tell by watching a skilled stylist work, by reading an expert's detailed breakdown)

**What undermines expertise:**
- Content that contradicts well-established expert consensus on YMYL topics
- Poor writing with obvious gaps in knowledge ("Popping pimples could be or could be not the new trend…")
- Medical or legal advice pages with no author information and no verifiable credentials
- Content that reads as if assembled from templates with no domain knowledge injected

### Expertise Varies by Topic

Think about what knowledge is actually required for the page to achieve its purpose. A photography hobby page requires different expertise than a page about tax preparation, which requires different expertise than a page about treating cancer. There is no one-size-fits-all expertise standard.

## 5. How to Assess Authoritativeness

Authoritativeness is about **reputation and recognition as a go-to source**, not just about being knowledgeable.

**Strong authority signals:**
- Official government or regulatory body pages (e.g., IRS for tax forms, NIH for BMI calculator)
- The brand or creator being the uniquely authoritative, go-to source (e.g., a software company's own documentation for its products; a national park's official website for park information)
- The only federally mandated source for a service (e.g., AnnualCreditReport.com for free credit reports in the US)
- Mainstream media institutions with long track records of credible journalism
- Institutions recognized in their field (e.g., top-rated hospitals per US News & World Report for medical information)
- Websites or content creators known as one of the best sources for a specific topic type

**Contextual authority signals:**
- A local business's own website is authoritative about itself (hours, menu, address)
- A niche forum is authoritative for that niche's practitioners
- A well-known satire/humor publication is authoritative for humorous content

**What authoritativeness is not:**
- Simply claiming expertise ("We are experts in…")
- Having a professional-looking website
- Being authoritative on one topic does not make a website authoritative on another (e.g., a cooking website is not authoritative for tax form downloads)

## 6. How to Assess Trust

Trust is the **most important** dimension and the central criterion. Pages that fail on Trust fail on E-E-A-T entirely, regardless of experience, expertise, or authority.

### Trust Signals (Positive)

**Transparency and information about the creator:**
- Clear "About Us" or author bio with verifiable information
- Contact information appropriate to the site type
- For stores: clear payment security, return policies, customer service channels
- For medical/financial/legal: verifiable credentials, institutional affiliations, peer-reviewed references

**Content quality:**
- Accuracy consistent with well-established expert consensus
- Citations and sources for factual claims
- Honest disclosures (e.g., affiliate link disclosures that don't undermine independent assessment)
- Balanced, helpful information written to benefit the user, not just the website owner

**Reputation research (external validation):**
- Independent reviews, references, news articles, and credible third-party sources
- Absence of reports of fraud, scams, or harmful behavior
- Recognition by professional societies or prestigious awards for YMYL topics

### Trust Signals (Negative — triggers Low or Lowest rating)

- **Complete lack of information** about who is responsible for the website/content on YMYL pages
- **Conflict of interest**: manufacturer "reviewing" their own product; paid promotion presented as independent review
- **Exaggerated or mildly misleading** claims of expertise or experience
- **Deceptive page design**: Ads designed to look like MC; fake survey pages; pages claiming to be something they are not
- **Characteristics of scams**: pages collecting sensitive personal information, fake checkout flows, impersonating official sources
- **Content contradicting well-established expert consensus** on medical, scientific, or safety topics
- **Factual inaccuracies** that suggest the creator doesn't know the topic

### Additional Trust Considerations Not Captured by E-E-A

- Customer service information for online stores
- Peer-reviewed publications for academic authors
- Secure payment systems for financial transaction websites
- Presence or absence of harmful intent (harm to people overrides all other Trust considerations)

## 7. Creator E-E-A-T vs. Website E-E-A-T

Both must be assessed separately, and both matter. For the overall page E-E-A-T assessment, consider who is actually responsible for the content.

### Website E-E-A-T
The overall reputation, purpose, and trustworthiness of the website as an entity.

- Does the website have a known, positive reputation for this type of content?
- Is the website the official, authoritative source for this information?
- Does the website have a history of accuracy, editorial standards, and reliability?
- Is there clear information about the organization behind the website?

### Content Creator E-E-A-T
The individual or team who actually authored the specific content on the page.

- Does the author have the experience, expertise, or credentials to write authoritatively on this topic?
- Is the author known and respected in their field?
- Are there independent sources (other publications, professional recognition, awards) confirming the creator's credibility?

### When They Differ

A high-reputation website can host content by an author with low E-E-A-T (e.g., a prestigious newspaper running a column by someone with no expertise in the topic). Evaluate the specific page and its specific author, not just the site's general reputation.

Conversely, a less-known website can host content by a highly credible expert. The content creator's E-E-A-T matters independently.

### Identifying Who Created the Content

| Webpage Type | Primary Content Creator |
|---|---|
| Business homepage, product page | The website itself (website owner is responsible) |
| Newspaper article, magazine piece, journal paper | The named author(s) — journalists, scientists, editorial board |
| Social media post, video channel | The individual or organization who created the account |
| Forum discussion, Q&A thread | The individual users posting; no single creator |

**How to find the creator:**
1. Look for a byline on the article/page
2. Check "About Us" or "About the Author" sections
3. Look at the content creator's profile page
4. Check what others say about the creator via independent reviews, references, or news
5. Examine the content itself for visible signals of expertise or experience
6. Note: aliases and usernames are acceptable identifiers for forums and social media

## 8. Everyday Expertise — When Formal Credentials Are Not Required

For many topics, life experience and practical knowledge are the primary E-E-A-T factors — not formal credentials.

**Topics where everyday expertise qualifies:**
- Parenting tips and personal experiences
- Recipes and cooking
- Hobbies (knitting, photography, gaming, gardening)
- Reviewing consumer products after personal use
- Sharing personal experiences with illness, relationships, or life events
- Humor and entertainment
- Travel descriptions based on first-hand visits

**Why everyday expertise matters:**
- Social media posts and forum discussions are often High quality when they involve people sharing genuine experience
- From writing symphonies to reviewing home appliances, first-hand experience can make a post High quality
- "Experience rather than formal expertise is enough" for humorous or personal content types

**Limits of everyday expertise:**
- Clear YMYL topics requiring factual advice (medical dosages, legal procedures, investment strategies, tax filing instructions) must come from genuine experts, not just people with personal experience
- Sharing a personal YMYL experience (e.g., coping with an illness) is different from giving advice (e.g., which treatment to pursue)

## 9. High vs. Low E-E-A-T Examples

### Strong E-E-A-T Examples

| Content | Why It Has Strong E-E-A-T |
|---|---|
| NIH BMI calculator | Official US government health institute; expert, authoritative, and trustworthy |
| IRS tax forms page | Uniquely authoritative for US tax information; government source |
| Kernel.org Linux archives | The go-to authoritative repository for Linux kernel source code |
| AnnualCreditReport.com | Only federally mandated source for free US credit reports |
| National Park official website | Uniquely authoritative for that park's information |
| Top-rated hospital's health information | Reputation confirmed by independent ratings (US News & World Report) |
| Award-winning newspaper's investigative journalism | Positive reputation, professional journalistic standards, expert reporters |
| Recipe blog author with documented extensive experimentation | Personal experience demonstrated through quantity and depth of unique original content |
| Fact-checking website with expertise in debunking urban legends | Recognized reputation for that specific skill; extensive track record |
| Forum post by knitter with 20+ years of sock-knitting experience | Informal expertise visible in the quality and depth of the post |
| Q&A thread where people describe how long a loved one lived with cancer | Experience-based content on a YMYL topic; trustworthy because it's honest personal experience, not advice |

### Weak / Low E-E-A-T Examples

| Content | Why It Has Weak E-E-A-T |
|---|---|
| Medical article with no author information and advice contradicting standard guidance | No verifiable expertise; contradicts expert consensus on YMYL topic |
| YMYL financial advice article with no information about who wrote it | Lowest: inadequate transparency about creator on a high-trust topic |
| Praying mantis article with factually inaccurate, nonsensical statements | Lowest: content quality suggests no expert knowledge; likely auto-generated |
| Poorly written article about stomach flu with non-standard medical advice | Lowest: contradicts expert consensus; no author credentials visible |
| Pimple-popping article opening with "could be or could be not" | Lowest: writing quality itself signals no skin care expertise |
| Product reviews paraphrased from other sources with no original content | Low: lacks first-hand experience and effort |
| Tax form downloads on a cooking website | Low: the website has no authority for that topic type |
| Shopping page with minimal customer service information | Low: inadequate Trust signals for a financial transaction context |
| Restaurant review by someone who never ate at the restaurant | Low: lacks the required Experience |

## 10. E-E-A-T by Topic Type

E-E-A-T standards vary significantly by topic. YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) topics require significantly higher scrutiny.

| Topic Type | Key E-E-A-T Considerations |
|---|---|
| **Medical / Health** | Expert authorship (licensed physicians, medical researchers). Content must align with well-established medical consensus. Trustworthy institutions (NIH, top hospitals). No contradictions of expert consensus even if creator has personal experience. |
| **Financial** | Expert authorship (CPAs, financial advisors, government agencies). Accuracy on matters that affect financial security. Official sources (IRS, regulated financial institutions) have highest authority. Conflict of interest (e.g., products being "reviewed" by sellers) is disqualifying. |
| **Legal** | Licensed attorneys or established legal institutions. Jurisdiction-specific accuracy matters. |
| **News and journalism** | Professional journalists; editorial standards; original reporting; institutional reputation. Citations to primary sources increase Trust. |
| **Safety and civic topics** | High accuracy requirements. Information that could cause people to miss voting, misunderstand safety procedures, or engage in dangerous behavior is subject to Lowest rating. |
| **Entertainment / Humor** | Formal credentials not required. Talent, originality, and audience recognition matter. Experience with the subject (e.g., cat ownership for a cat video) can be sufficient. |
| **Hobbies and crafts** | Informal expertise through practice and demonstrated skill. Forum communities with engaged practitioners often have high E-E-A-T. |
| **Shopping / e-commerce** | Trustworthiness signals: secure payment, return policies, customer service. Reviews must be independent, not manufacturer-generated. |
| **Personal experience / life events** | First-hand experience is the primary E-E-A-T factor. Formal credentials less important. As long as the content is honest and doesn't give harmful advice, high E-E-A-T is achievable. |
| **Technical / software documentation** | The company or creator of the software/product is uniquely authoritative for its own documentation. |
| **Academic / scientific** | Peer-reviewed publication standards. Citations, institutional affiliation, methodology transparency. |

## 11. "Who Is Responsible for This Page" — How to Find and Evaluate the Creator

### Step 1: Identify the Website Owner
- Navigate to the homepage (follow the logo or site header link)
- Look for "About Us," "About," or "Contact" pages
- Look for clear information about the company, organization, or individual behind the site

### Step 2: Identify the Content Creator
- Look for a byline on articles or posts
- Check for author bio boxes (common on blogs, newspapers)
- For social media or forums: the posting account or username is the creator
- For product pages: the website itself is the creator

### Step 3: Research Reputation Independently

**Search operator technique** (what Google actually teaches raters to use):

To find independent information about a site, exclude the site itself from results:
- `[example.com -site:example.com]` — mentions of the domain anywhere except the site itself
- `["example.com" -site:example.com]` — same, quoted for exactness
- `[example.com reviews -site:example.com]` — reviews specifically
- `["example.com" reviews -site:example.com]` — quoted + reviews

For a content creator by name, substitute the site URL with the creator's name.

**What to look for:**
- Independent reviews, news articles, references by others
- Recognition by professional organizations or industry awards
- Reports of fraud, scams, deceptive behavior, or harm
- For non-professional creators: comments from other creators or community members about them

**Customer reviews — how to interpret them:**
- Be skeptical of both positive AND negative reviews — anyone can write them
- Read the *content* of reviews, not just the star rating or total count
- Any legitimate business gets some negative reviews — this is normal and neutral
- What matters: **credible patterns** of reports describing specific harm (fraud, undelivered goods, harmful advice, deceptive practices)
- A handful of negative reviews among hundreds of positive ones ≠ a reputation problem
- Absence of reviews for a small/new site = neutral, not negative

### Step 4: Verify Credentials Through the Content Itself
- Does the content demonstrate genuine knowledge?
- Are there factual errors, inconsistencies, or implausible claims?
- Does the tone and depth match what an expert or experienced person would produce?
- Are claims supported by sources or peer-reviewed references where appropriate?

### Red Flags
- No "About Us," no author information, no contact information on a YMYL site
- AI-generated author profiles with fake credentials or stock photos
- Terms of service that contradict the stated purpose of the site
- Exaggerated claims of expertise that cannot be verified externally
- Credentials that are claimed but not verifiable

### Acceptable Identifiers
- Aliases and usernames on forums and social media platforms are acceptable
- Personal websites may omit home address/phone for legitimate privacy reasons
- A long-standing Internet alias can identify a content creator

## 12. E-E-A-T Scoring Rubric

### Highest (Very High E-E-A-T)

**Criteria — must demonstrate at least one:**
- The website or content creator is the uniquely authoritative, go-to source for the topic (e.g., official government page, manufacturer's own documentation, the only authorized source for a service)
- Very positive reputation confirmed by independent, external sources; recognized as one of the best sources for this type of content
- Content creator has a wealth of experience and/or very high expertise; independently recognized (awards, professional societies, peer recognition)

**Characteristics:**
- Trust is very high; no significant credibility concerns
- Reputation confirmed externally (not just self-claimed)
- For YMYL topics: consistent with expert consensus, appropriate credentials visible

---

### Strong / High E-E-A-T

**Criteria:**
- Satisfying level of experience or expertise for the topic
- Positive (not necessarily exceptional) reputation for the topic
- Clear information about who is responsible for the content
- Content is accurate, honest, and written to benefit users

**Characteristics:**
- Appropriate expertise visible in the content itself
- No significant conflicts of interest
- Trust indicators present (contact info, author bio, source citations as appropriate)
- For YMYL: expertise is present and content aligns with expert consensus

---

### Adequate / Medium E-E-A-T

**Criteria:**
- Some signals of experience or expertise but not exceptional
- No clearly negative reputation but limited reputation information
- Reasonable information about the creator available
- Content is not harmful but not outstanding

**Characteristics:**
- "Nothing wrong, nothing special"
- May lack depth or originality but is not inaccurate
- For YMYL: requires more scrutiny; adequate for non-YMYL

---

### Weak / Low E-E-A-T

**Criteria — any one is sufficient:**
- Content creator lacks adequate experience for the topic (e.g., review without having used the product)
- Content creator lacks adequate expertise for the topic (e.g., medical advice with no medical background)
- Website is not an authoritative or trustworthy source for the topic (e.g., tax forms on a cooking site)
- Page or website is not trustworthy for its purpose (e.g., shopping page with no customer service info)
- Exaggerated or mildly misleading claims of expertise
- Mildly negative reputation

**Key rule:** No other positive factor (positive reputation, professional-looking website) can overcome a lack of E-E-A-T for the topic or purpose of the page.

---

### Lowest E-E-A-T

**Criteria — any one is sufficient:**
- Page or website is highly untrustworthy
- Complete lack of information about who is responsible for the content on a YMYL or high-trust page
- Content contains harmful inaccuracies contradicting well-established expert consensus
- Deceptive design, fake credentials, or misrepresentation of purpose
- Very negative reputation (fraud, scams, harmful behavior)
- Content that could cause real harm to people (dangerous medical advice, financial scam, hate content)

**Key rule:** Harm assessment happens first and overrides all other considerations. If a page is harmful, it is Lowest regardless of apparent expertise or experience.

## Quick Reference Checklist

When evaluating E-E-A-T, work through these questions:

**Experience**
- [ ] Has the creator personally done/used/lived what they are writing about?
- [ ] Is there specific detail that only comes from first-hand experience?
- [ ] Or is the content clearly assembled from secondary sources with no personal contribution?

**Expertise**
- [ ] Does the topic require formal expertise (YMYL) or everyday expertise?
- [ ] Does the content demonstrate the appropriate level of knowledge?
- [ ] Are there visible errors or gaps that signal missing expertise?
- [ ] Can the creator's credentials be independently verified?

**Authoritativeness**
- [ ] Is this the official, go-to source for this type of information?
- [ ] Does the website have a recognized positive reputation for this topic?
- [ ] What do independent sources say about this creator/website?

**Trust**
- [ ] Is there adequate information about who is responsible for this content?
- [ ] Is the content accurate and consistent with expert consensus (especially for YMYL)?
- [ ] Are there conflicts of interest affecting objectivity?
- [ ] Is the page honest about its purpose?
- [ ] Does the site have appropriate customer service / contact information for its type?
