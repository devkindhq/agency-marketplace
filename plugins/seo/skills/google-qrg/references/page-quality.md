# Page Quality (PQ) Rating Reference

Source: Google Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines (2025 edition), Sections 3–8.
Use this as a runtime reference when assigning PQ ratings.

---

## 1. The 5-Tier PQ Scale

The scale runs: **Lowest | Low | Medium | High | Highest**
In-between ratings (+½ steps) are also used: Lowest+, Low+, Medium+, High+

### Rating workflow (always run in order)
1. Does the page have a **harmful purpose** or **deceive about its true purpose**? → Lowest
2. Does the page have **potential to cause harm** (harmful MC, harmful to groups, harmfully misleading)? → Lowest
3. Otherwise, rate based on how well the page achieves its purpose

---

## 2. Lowest Quality

### Core definition
Untrustworthy, deceptive, harmful to people or society, or spammy. **Any single criterion below justifies Lowest.**

### Lowest — Quality of MC
- Page is hacked, defaced, or spammed
- MC is gibberish or makes no sense
- MC created with little/no effort, no originality, no added value vs. similar pages on the web
- MC so low-effort that the page fails to achieve its purpose

### Lowest — Page Title
- Title is extremely misleading, shocking, or exaggerated

### Lowest — Ads/SC
- MC is deliberately obstructed/obscured by Ads, SC, interstitials, or download links that benefit the site owner at the expense of the visitor

### Lowest — Site/Creator Information
- Complete lack of information about who is responsible for the site (especially on YMYL pages or pages handling sensitive data)

### Lowest — Reputation
- Very negative reputation including malicious or harmful behavior

### Lowest — E-E-A-T
- Page or website is highly untrustworthy
- Lack of experience/expertise/authoritativeness/trust causes the page to fail its purpose

## 3. Types of Lowest Pages

### 3a. Harmful to Self or Other Individuals
Pages that encourage, depict, incite, or directly cause physical, mental, emotional, or financial harm.

**Red flags:**
- Incitement of violence toward individuals
- Serious/realistic death threats
- Doxxing (exposing personal info with malicious intent)
- How-to instructions for committing violent acts (easy-to-replicate format)
- Glorifying or trivializing violence or atrocities
- Suicide promotion or pro-anorexia content
- Health advice contradicting well-established expert consensus that could prevent life-saving treatment
- Encouraging unsafe behavior while substantially downplaying risks (e.g., consuming household chemicals)

**NOT harmful:** News stories about violence, educational content, fictional entertainment, advocacy against domestic violence, portrayals of dangerous activities that clearly explain risks

### 3b. Harmful to Specified Groups
Pages promoting, condoning, or inciting hatred against groups defined by:
Age, Caste, Disability, Ethnicity, Gender Identity/Expression, Immigration Status, Nationality, Race, Religion, Sex/Gender, Sexual Orientation, Veteran Status, Victims of major violent events, or any other characteristic associated with systemic discrimination.

**Tone must be serious or mean-spirited** (not satirical/joking) to qualify.

**Harmful examples:**
- Encourages violence or ill treatment toward a Specified Group
- Promotes intolerance by refusing to allow the views/beliefs/behavior of a Specified Group
- Implies one group is superior/inferior to another
- Contains extremely offensive/dehumanizing stereotypes (positive or negative)

**NOT harmful:** Historical documentaries featuring hateful speeches, stand-up comedy that is not mean-spirited, newspaper articles about hate organizations, dictionary definitions of slurs, discussions of religious texts

### 3c. Harmfully Misleading Information
Misleads people in ways that can cause harm. Applies even to seemingly expert/authoritative sources.

Three types — all require Lowest:
1. **Harmful and clearly inaccurate information** — refutable by straightforward widely accepted facts (e.g., false claim a world leader died, false election dates)
2. **Harmful claims contradicting well-established expert consensus** (e.g., "lemons cure cancer", "buying lottery tickets is a guaranteed way to save for retirement")
3. **Harmful unsubstantiated theories/claims** not grounded in reasonable facts/evidence, especially those that erode confidence in public institutions (e.g., "9/11 was planned by the US government", "world leaders are lizard people")

**NOT harmfully misleading:** Non-YMYL entertainment/satire/fiction, personal opinion reviews, reasonably debatable claims with no single correct answer, trivial factual errors about inconsequential topics

### 3d. Untrustworthy Webpages
**Signals of untrustworthiness:**
- Multiple/significant factual inaccuracies on an informational page
- Inadequate information about website/creator for its purpose
- Lowest E-E-A-T or extremely negative reputation
- Deceptive purpose, design, or intent
- Deliberately obstructed/obscured MC
- Scam characteristics, malicious downloads, or harmful behavior
- Designed to manipulate people into actions benefiting the site while harming users

**Deceptive purpose examples:**
- Product recommendation page falsely impersonating a celebrity blog
- "Independent review" secretly created by the manufacturer
- Page impersonating another site (copied logo/branding, URL mimicking another site's name)
- Non-satirical impersonator profile on social media
- News-looking site publishing articles to manipulate users for political/financial benefit
- "Fake" owner/creator profiles (AI-generated images, fabricated credentials, false medical credentials)

**Deceptive design examples:**
- Ads disguised as MC (fake search results pages, fake directory pages)
- Deceptively designed buttons/links (e.g., "close" button that downloads an app)
- Misleading page title with no connection to actual content

**Deliberately obstructed MC examples:**
- Ads that continue covering MC as users scroll, virtually impossible to close
- Pop-ups that cannot be closed without taking an action that benefits the site
- Interstitial pages coercing downloads or clicks
- Ads pushing MC so far down most users won't find it
- MC in white-on-white text or otherwise hidden from human reading

**Suspected malicious behavior:**
- Pages strongly suspected to be scams
- Pages asking for personal info without legitimate reason (name, birthdate, SSN, bank account, ATM PIN)
- Pages appearing to phish for passwords (Facebook, Gmail, etc.)
- Links strongly suspected to be malware downloads

### 3e. Spammy Webpages

**No MC / gibberish / purposeless pages:**
- Purpose cannot be determined because MC is gibberish
- Page deliberately has no MC
- MC so minimal it fails to achieve any purpose
- MC so low quality it fails to achieve any purpose
- MC adds no value vs. similar pages on the web

**Hacked/defaced/spammed pages:**
- Website modified without owner permission
- Forum pages or comments sections filled with unrelated spam posts advertising products or creating links

**Expired domain abuse:**
- Expired domain purchased and repurposed with low-value content benefiting the new owner
- URL looks like a trusted source (government, charity, school) but content is completely unrelated (payday loans, gambling, casino content)
- Verify using Internet Archive if suspicious

**Site reputation abuse:**
- Third-party content published on a host site primarily to inherit that host's established ranking signals, not to serve users
- Examples: educational site hosting sponsored payday loan reviews; medical site hosting "best casinos" page; news site hosting supplement maker's article as if editorial
- NOT abuse: wire/press release services, syndicated news between news publications, user forums/comment sections, editorial columns, appropriately treated affiliate links, coupons sourced directly from merchants

**Scaled content abuse:**
- Many pages generated with little effort/originality to benefit the site owner, not help users
- Using AI/automated tools to produce large volumes of low-value pages
- Scraping, synonymizing, translating, or otherwise obfuscating existing content at scale
- Stitching/combining content from different pages without adding value
- Creating many sites to hide the scaled nature of content
- Pages with content making little/no sense to a reader but containing search keywords

**Copied/paraphrased content with no added value (Lowest when all or almost all MC is copied with little/no effort/originality/value):**
- Exact copying/scraping
- Copied with minimal alteration (find-and-replace, minor edits)
- Copies of dynamic content (search results, news feeds)
- Paraphrased from one or multiple sources by person or AI
- Note: Licensed/syndicated content (AP, Reuters) is NOT "copied"
- Note: Generative AI alone does not determine quality — AI can produce both Lowest and Highest quality content

## 4. Low Quality

### Core definition
Has a beneficial or non-harmful purpose. Does not meet Lowest standards. Fails to achieve its purpose well, or has problematic aspects. **Any single criterion below justifies Low.**

### Low — Quality of MC
- MC created without adequate effort, originality, talent, or skill to achieve the page's purpose in a satisfying way
- Mild inaccuracies on informational pages (but not harmful enough for Lowest)

### Low — Page Title
- Slightly misleading, shocking, or exaggerated

### Low — Ads/SC
- Ads or SC significantly distract from or interrupt use of the MC
- Single pop-over with clear close button is acceptable; difficult-to-close scroll-following ads or interstitials requiring app downloads are not
- Sexually suggestive, grotesque, or shocking Ad/SC images on otherwise non-sexual pages

### Low — Site/Creator Information
- Unsatisfying amount of website info or content creator info for the purpose
- Stores processing financial transactions: insufficient customer service info or contact info
- YMYL pages: insufficient info about who is responsible

### Low — Reputation
- Mildly negative reputation (not malicious or fraudulent)

### Low — E-E-A-T
- Inadequate level of E-E-A-T for the purpose of the page
- Cannot be overcome by positive reputation or site type

### Low E-E-A-T examples:
- Content creator lacks adequate experience (e.g., restaurant review by someone who never ate there)
- Content creator lacks adequate expertise (e.g., skydiving article by non-expert)
- Website not authoritative for the topic (e.g., tax forms from a cooking website)
- Shopping page with minimal customer service info

### Low Quality MC examples:
- Crafting tutorial with lots of unhelpful filler at top, vague instructions
- "Best vacuum cleaners" list with paraphrased reviews, minimal original content
- Slideshow of world destinations using others' pictures and existing lists
- Page titled "How many cm in a meter?" with mostly off-topic filler
- List of tallest mountains missing Mount Everest
- Large amount of visually prominent filler making it hard to find helpful MC
- Recipe page requiring significant scrolling past ads/filler to reach the actual recipe

### Low Quality title examples:
- "Pink Elephant: Part 2 coming soon!" — but content is just speculation/rumor
- "Is the World about to End? Mysterious Sightings of Sea Serpents Prompt Panic!" — for an article about a dead fish
- "Eat the Healthiest Foods in the WORLD to extend your life!!!!" — for a basic nutrition tips list

### Low effort/originality/added value (Low, not Lowest, when much but not all MC is copied):
- Social media reposts with little additional comment
- Pages of embedded videos or "repinned" images with little curation or discussion
- "Best of" lists based on existing reviews with little original content

### Exaggerated creator info:
- Claims of personal experience or expertise that seem overstated or unverifiable
- "I'm an expert!" claims not supported by MC, credentials, or external reputation info

## 5. Medium Quality

### Core definition
Beneficial purpose, achieves that purpose. Nothing wrong with the page. Common online — expect to encounter many Medium pages.

### Two types of Medium pages:
1. **Nothing wrong, but nothing special** — all Medium criteria met; adequate but not exceptional
2. **Mixed, but with some redeeming qualities** — some High quality signals, but also one sign or mild signs of Low quality

### Medium criteria:
- MC created with adequate effort, originality, talent, or skill — page achieves its purpose
- Reputation: not especially positive or negative; not concerning, but not positive enough for High
- Adequate E-E-A-T for the purpose of the page
- Title summarizes the page
- Ads/SC do not block or significantly interfere with MC
- Adequate information about website and content creator

### Medium examples:
- Short Wikipedia article on a narrow topic (achieves purpose, lacks depth/effort for High)
- Entertainment news article about a TV show (achieves purpose, no exceptional effort)
- Recipe contributed by a cookbook author but with no user reviews or related SC
- Humorous site with photos and user engagement but no discussion in comments

## 6. High Quality

### Core definition
Serves a beneficial purpose and achieves it **well**. Must demonstrate at least ONE of:
- MC created with a **high level** of effort, originality, talent, or skill
- **Positive reputation** of website/content creator for the topic
- **High level of E-E-A-T** for the purpose of the page

### High Quality MC signals:
- High level of effort: well-organized, edited, curated; news article with accuracy/depth/clarity; meaningful Q&A discussion; clear crafting tutorial
- High originality: unique content not available elsewhere; original photos/video; personal perspectives from first-hand experience
- High talent or skill: content showcases creator's talent; how-to by a skilled practitioner
- For informational pages: accurate and consistent with well-established expert consensus
- Note: "typical" and "average" pages on a topic are usually Medium, not High

### Positive reputation (High):
- Positive reputation of website for the topic
- Positive reputation of content creator for the MC
- For non-YMYL: popularity, user engagement, user reviews count as reputation signals
- Smaller websites and ordinary people may have little reputation info — this alone does not prevent High

### High E-E-A-T signals:
- Experience: social media/forum posts where people share genuine first-hand experience; product reviews by actual users
- Expertise: content from people with verifiable skills (formal or informal) appropriate to the topic
- Authoritative: government pages for their official info; local businesses as go-to sources for local info; known experts in a field
- Trust: particularly important for financial transactions and YMYL topics

### High examples:
- Homepage of an award-winning newspaper (original reporting, professional journalists, positive reputation)
- US Naval Observatory Master Clock page (authoritative source for official time)
- Satirical humor article on a well-known humor website (talent, skill, positive reputation)
- Local restaurant "about us" page with address, menu, contact info (authoritative for itself)
- Backpack shopping page on a reputable merchant with user reviews and detailed customer service info
- SNL episode on video website (original content, talented organization)
- Q&A forum post with meaningful discussion from multiple participants with first-hand experience

---

## 7. Highest Quality

### Core definition
Serves a beneficial purpose and achieves it **very well**. Distinguished from High by exceptional quality. Must demonstrate at least ONE of:
- MC created with a **very high level** of effort, originality, talent, or skill
- **Very positive reputation** of website/content creator
- **Very high level of E-E-A-T**

### Highest Quality MC:
- Highly satisfying; outstanding compared to other content available on the topic
- For informational pages: accurate, clearly communicated, comprehensive, consistent with expert consensus

**By content type:**
- **News:** Original investigative reporting revealing information not otherwise known; describes primary sources; meets professional journalistic standards; accurate and in-depth
- **Artistic content (videos, images, writing):** Unique, original content by highly skilled/talented artists; high skill/effort required. If YMYL-related (e.g., informing opinion on YMYL topics), apply YMYL standards
- **Informational content:** Original, accurate, comprehensive, clearly communicated; reflects expert consensus as appropriate; meets field-specific standards

### Very positive reputation (Highest):
- For YMYL: based on recommendations from known experts or professional societies; prestigious awards
- For non-YMYL: seen as one of the best sources available online for the topic
- No reputation info available does not prevent Highest for pages that otherwise qualify

### Very high E-E-A-T (Highest):
- Website/content creator is the **uniquely authoritative, go-to source** for the topic
- Content creator with a **wealth of experience** where experience is the primary trust factor
- **Very high expertise** in the relevant field
- These are the most trusted sources on the internet for the particular topic

## 8. YMYL Pages — Stricter Evaluation

### What YMYL means
"Your Money or Your Life" — topics that could significantly impact:
- The person viewing the content
- Others affected by the person who viewed the content
- Groups of people or society affected by actions of those who viewed the content

### YMYL categories:
- **Health or Safety:** Mental, physical, emotional health, or any safety (physical, online)
- **Financial Security:** Topics that could damage ability to support oneself/family
- **Government, Civics & Society:** Issues of public interest, trust in public institutions, elections, voting, civic topics impacting people's lives
- **Other:** Any topic that could hurt people or negatively impact societal welfare

### YMYL spectrum:
- **Clear YMYL:** Tsunami evacuation routes, when to go to the ER, prescription drug purchases, advice on treating specific diseases, tax filing, investing, voting eligibility
- **May be YMYL:** Weather forecasts, how often to replace a toothbrush, car reviews
- **Not/unlikely YMYL:** Music award winners, how frequently to wash jeans, a rock band opinion

### Litmus test for YMYL:
1. Would a careful person seek out experts or highly trusted sources to prevent harm? Could even minor inaccuracies cause harm? → Likely YMYL
2. Is the topic something most people would casually discuss with friends? → Likely not YMYL

### How YMYL affects PQ rating:
- Clear YMYL pages require the **highest scrutiny**
- For YMYL informational pages: accuracy and consistency with well-established expert consensus is mandatory
- Inexpert content on YMYL topics → Untrustworthy → Lowest
- No information about who created YMYL content → Lowest
- Low E-E-A-T on YMYL topics → Low rating at minimum
- Reputation for YMYL topics must be judged by **experts in the field** (professional societies, peer-reviewed publications)

### YMYL — Experience vs. Expertise distinction:
- If the purpose is **to give information or advice on a YMYL topic**: high level of **expertise** required
- If the purpose is **to share personal experience on a YMYL topic**: first-hand **experience** may be sufficient, as long as content is trustworthy, safe, and consistent with well-established expert consensus

| YMYL Topic | Experience OK | Expertise Required |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep challenges when pregnant | Non-medical tips from people who experienced this (safe sleeping positions) | Sleep medications safe during pregnancy |
| Liver cancer treatment | Forum discussion about coping with treatment | Different treatment options and life expectancies |
| Filling out tax forms | Humorous video about tax frustration | Instructions on how to fill out forms |
| Saving for retirement | Reviews of retirement services by users | Investment advice, amounts, asset types |
| How to vote | Social media post about why voting matters | Who is eligible to vote, how to register |

## 9. Specific Page Type Evaluations

### News pages
- **High/Highest:** Original reporting by professional journalists; award-winning publications; accurate, in-depth, clearly sourced content
- **Low:** Grammar/punctuation errors; paraphrased from a science article with introduced inaccuracies; misleading title not matching article content
- **Lowest:** Fake "news" site publishing deliberately inaccurate information (e.g., false death claim); no date, no author, no contact info

### Articles/Blog posts
- **High:** Expert author with verifiable credentials; original perspective; accurate and well-cited
- **Low:** Mostly commonly-known information, no expertise evidence; large amounts of filler; garbled or meaningless content; unrelated links mid-article
- **Lowest:** Auto-generated with no human editing; odd factual inaccuracies suggesting non-human authorship

### Recipe pages
- **High:** Clear instructions, original photos, user reviews, well-organized with recipe prominently placed
- **Low:** Recipe missing critical information (e.g., no crust instructions for apple pie); MC buried under ads/filler requiring extensive scrolling; distracting/shocking unrelated ad images
- Medium: Recipe that works and achieves purpose but has no user reviews, no SC related to purpose

### Product/Shopping pages
- **High/Highest:** Well-known reputable merchant; detailed customer service info; user reviews; products unique to site; secure payment indicated
- **Low:** Insufficient customer service or contact info; inconsistent shipping/return policies; misuse of trust logos
- **Lowest:** Scam merchant characteristics; physically threatened users; financial fraud reports; minimal contact info for a financial-transaction site

### Homepages
- **High:** Award-winning news homepage with original reporting; clear site identity and purpose
- **Lowest:** White supremacist or extremist site homepage; expired domain now hosting gambling content; site impersonating a trusted organization

### Forums/Q&A/Social media pages
- Content creator identified by alias/username is acceptable
- **High:** Meaningful discussion with multiple participants sharing first-hand experience
- **Low:** Forum post with only superficial comments; little discussion
- **Lowest:** Spammed comment sections; bots posting unrelated product links

### Government/Official pages
- **High/Highest:** US Naval Observatory clock page (authoritative for time); official passport renewal page (uniquely authoritative for the topic)
- **Lowest if:** Hacked and repurposed (e.g., government site now selling Xanax)

### Medical pages
- Require high E-E-A-T — ideally healthcare professionals or institutions with expert reputation
- **Lowest:** No author info on YMYL medical site; content contradicting standard medical guidance; content encouraging dangerous health behaviors
- **Low:** No evidence of author medical expertise; mild inaccuracies; superficial "commonly known info" only

### Financial pages
- Require high E-E-A-T and trustworthiness for any transactional or advisory content
- **Low:** Superficial investment advice with no evidence of financial expertise
- **Lowest:** Scam debt relief schemes; fake payment pages collecting SSNs/bank account numbers

### Encyclopedia/Reference pages
- **High:** Civil War encyclopedia article with in-depth content and citations
- **Medium:** Short Wikipedia article on a narrow topic — achieves purpose but lacks depth/effort for High
- **Low:** Missing key facts (e.g., tallest mountains list without Everest); excessive filler relative to actual information

## 10. E-E-A-T Quick Reference

| Signal | What it means | How to assess |
|---|---|---|
| **Experience** | First-hand or life experience with the topic | Has the creator personally used/done/experienced this? |
| **Expertise** | Knowledge or skill for the topic | Credentials, demonstrated knowledge in MC, professional background |
| **Authoritativeness** | Recognized go-to source for the topic | Independent mentions, citations, professional society recognition, awards |
| **Trust** | Accurate, honest, safe, reliable | Most important signal. Untrustworthy = low E-E-A-T regardless of E, E, or A |

**Trust by page type:**
- Online stores: secure payment + reliable customer service
- Product reviews: honest, written to help buyers (not just to sell)
- YMYL informational: accurate, expert-consistent
- Non-YMYL social media posts: may need only minimal trust if no harm risk

**Conflict of interest lowers trust:**
- Manufacturer "reviewing" their own product
- Paid influencer reviewing a product without disclosure
- Supplement maker's article published on a news site to inherit the site's reputation

## 11. Key Principles Summary

1. **Any single Lowest criterion** is sufficient for a Lowest rating — you do not need multiple.
2. **Presence of ads alone** is never grounds for a lower rating — sites need monetization.
3. **YMYL pages** require much higher scrutiny; Low E-E-A-T on YMYL = Low or Lowest.
4. **No reputation info** for a small/ordinary site is neutral, not negative.
5. **Generative AI content** is not automatically low quality; evaluate by effort, originality, and value.
6. **Copying/paraphrasing** is not automatically Lowest; evaluate the degree (all vs. some) and added value.
7. **Logins/paywalls** on otherwise trustworthy sites are not signs of untrustworthiness.
8. **Opinion, satire, fiction** on non-YMYL topics is not harmfully misleading.
9. **Mediocre but not wrong** = Medium, not Low. Low requires a specific failing.
10. **"Typical" content** on a topic is usually Medium; High requires notable quality signals.
